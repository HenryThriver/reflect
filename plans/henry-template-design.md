### Tiago Forte (28 questions, ~60 min)
**Unique angle:** Memory-focused, emotional, creative. "Remember. Connect. Create."
- Phase 1 - Preparation: calendar review, photo review, notes/second brain review
- Phase 2 - Remember: peak experiences, meaningful conversations, books/media, new people, risks taken, surprises, tough moments, proud of, grateful people, favorite places, new skills, creative work, favorite memories
- Phase 3 - Connect & Create: year story title + summary, lessons forward, let go, how to FEEL/BECOME/CREATE/EXPERIENCE/LEARN, word for next year, letter to future self, three priorities, first action, gratitude close

**Standout questions:**
- "If this year were a chapter in your life story, what would you title it?"
- "What risks did you take?"
- "Who are the new people you met that made an impression?"
- "How do you want to FEEL next year?" (feelings > achievements)

### Chris Guillebeau (not a template - thought process)
**Source:** https://chrisguillebeau.com/how-to-conduct-your-own-annual-review

**Core Framework:**
- Two foundational questions: "What went well?" and "What did not go well?"
- Generate 6-8 answers to each
- Only include outcomes you could actually influence (controllability principle)
- ~70-80% goal achievement is healthy (means you're being ambitious enough)

**Process:**
- Review previous year's goals in "Final Review" column
- Assess achievement level + understand why
- Category-based goal setting (Writing, Business, Health, Travel, Financial)
- 3-5 measurable goals per category
- "Required actions" column identifying necessary steps
- Year theme statement

**Key insight:** Simplicity + controllability + honesty

## Draft Architecture: Henry's Annual Review Template

### Design Principles
1. **Sparkline-informed** - Oscillate between "what is" (DOWN) and "what could be" (UP)
2. **Value Forest integrated** - Review centered on your 18 Value Trees
3. **Comprehensive** - Err on side of thorough (Gustin-style depth)
4. **Emotionally resonant** - Open warmly, process deeply, end with transformed commitment
5. **Language aligned** - Thriving, outcomes, resolves (not goals, success, targets)

### Section Details

## Duarte Sparkline Analysis

**Source:** Nancy Duarte's "Resonate" framework ([TEDx talk](https://www.ted.com/talks/nancy_duarte_the_secret_structure_of_great_talks), [Duarte blog](https://www.duarte.com/blog/ultimate-guide-to-contrast/))

### The Core Concept

The Sparkline is a visual representation of how great speeches and presentations alternate between two states:
- **"What Is"** (lower line) - The current reality, status quo, challenges, problems
- **"What Could Be"** (upper line) - The vision, possibility, aspiration, transformation

### How It Works

1. **Contrast creates tension** - The gap between "what is" and "what could be" should be as large as possible
2. **Oscillation maintains engagement** - You "tack" back and forth like a sailboat, not one straight line up
3. **The journey ends at "New Bliss"** - The transformed state where the audience has been persuaded to join you

## Final Structure

### Design Analysis: Core Tensions Navigated

**1. Comprehensive vs. Emergent**
- Want "no stone unturned" depth (Gustin-style)
- But also value serendipity, unexpected discoveries, emergence
- Ness Labs felt "surprisingly flat" despite feeling comprehensive
- TK felt "too prescriptive" with no room for emergence
- **Solution:** Structure creates containers for depth, but with open-ended prompts that allow unexpected insights. Not just "rate 1-10" but "what surprised you?"

**2. Human vs. Business-y**
- Strong aversion to OKRs, QBRs, corporate framing, "goals" language
- Loved MashaPlans' "feminine" feel - warm, welcoming
- Want spiritual/human framing (fear, rest, joy) over metrics
- Yet still want actionable outcomes and quarterly review feed-forward
- **Solution:** Frame everything in human terms (thriving, outcomes, resolves) but still extract practical commitments. The wolf shapes the river - not "set KPIs."

**3. Grounded in Data vs. Open to Feeling**
- Love grounding in specifics: actual calendar, photos, real data from last 4-8 weeks
- But equally value somatic/emotional questions: "How do you want to feel?"
- Revealed preferences (what you actually did) vs. cognitive beliefs (what you think you should do)
- **Solution:** Alternate between data-grounded and feeling-oriented questions. Use one to inform the other.

**4. Intentional Prep vs. Just Dive In**
- Strong emphasis on prep: closing open loops, setting container, explicit time commitment
- Neuroscience-backed intentionality matters
- **Solution:** Prep section is foundational, almost ritualistic - not optional housekeeping.

### What Lights Henry Up (Pattern Recognition)

1. **Memory-first approach** - "Arguably the most valuable because it surfaces things I wasn't aware of."
2. **Intellectual nimbleness** - "Where did I change my mind?" primes questioning beliefs gracefully
3. **Practical wisdom over theory** - "Who has solved this before? Who can help?"
4. **Small joys, not just big wins** - "Something that made you smile" alongside accomplishments
5. **Revealed preferences** - What you actually did/forgot matters more than what you thought you valued
6. **Rest as a category** - Explicitly examining what qualifies as effective rest
7. **Big questions** - Feynman's 12, dream-state visualizations, "meet yourself at 80"

### Three Emotional Registers (Use Throughout)

Every major section should touch all three:
1. **Data/Memory** - What actually happened (calendar, photos, revealed preferences)
2. **Feeling/Somatic** - How did it feel? How do you want to feel?
3. **Wisdom/Integration** - What does this mean? What did you learn?
